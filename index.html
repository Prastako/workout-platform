Ask Claude
The fundamental rule
Mechanical tension drives muscle growth. The muscle must be loaded under stretch and contraction — not just moved through space. A lighter weight felt intensely in the target muscle is almost always more productive than a heavier weight dominated by momentum and secondary movers.
Tempo & control
Eccentric (lowering)
Take 2–3 seconds on the way down. The muscle is strongest eccentrically — this phase causes the most fibre damage and therefore the most adaptation. Never drop the weight.
Concentric (lifting)
Drive with intent. You don't need to go slow — you need to feel the muscle doing the work. If your joints are taking over, the load is too heavy or your position is wrong.
Range of motion
Full range whenever anatomy permits. The stretched position (bottom of a rep) creates the highest tension and the greatest growth stimulus. Partial reps have a place but should not be the default.
Mind-muscle connection
Before each set, place a hand on the target muscle and deliberately contract it. During the set, focus on that muscle — not on completing the rep. If you can't feel it, reduce the weight until you can. The connection improves with practice and is worth building early.
Movement patterns to cover weekly
꧁
Push — horizontal (bench, dips) and vertical (overhead press)
꧁
Pull — horizontal (rows) and vertical (pulldowns, pull-ups)
꧁
Hinge — hip hinge patterns (deadlift, RDL, good morning)
꧁
Squat — bilateral (squat) and unilateral (lunge, split squat)
꧁
Carry — loaded walks, farmer carries, suitcase carries
Rest between sets
Hypertrophy: 60–90 seconds. Enough to recover, short enough to keep metabolic stress elevated.

Strength: 3–5 minutes. Full nervous system recovery is needed to express maximum force.

When in doubt, rest longer rather than shorter — incomplete recovery produces worse sets, not better conditioning.
⊹ Ask Claude — training
Going to failure
True muscular failure — the point where another full rep is physically impossible with good form — is one of the most potent growth signals available. Most people never reach it. They stop when it becomes uncomfortable, which is usually 3–5 reps before actual failure.
Reps in Reserve (RIR)
Use RIR to calibrate effort across sets. On a 3-set working block:

꧁
Set 1 — 2 RIR. Hard but clean. Saving enough to not pre-fatigue.
꧁
Set 2 — 1 RIR. One more rep would have been possible, barely.
꧁
Set 3 — 0 RIR. Failure. The last rep should be a grind.
Rep ranges
꧁
1–5 reps — Maximum strength. Heavy load, full recovery between sets (3–5 min), low total volume.
꧁
6–12 reps — Hypertrophy sweet spot. Moderate load, moderate rest. Most sessions should live here.
꧁
15–30 reps — Metabolic hypertrophy and endurance. Particularly useful for isolation work (side delts, calves, bicep curls).
All ranges build muscle. The most important variable is proximity to failure, not the exact rep count.
Progressive overload
The body adapts to a stimulus and then stops responding to it. You must regularly increase the demand — more weight, more reps, more sets, or less rest — to keep progressing. Track your lifts. If you're not beating your previous numbers over time, you're not overloading.
Deload
Every 4–8 weeks, drop volume by 40–50% for one week. Keep intensity (weight) the same, reduce sets. This is not optional for long-term progress — it allows connective tissue and the nervous system to recover from accumulated fatigue that muscles don't signal directly.
⊹ Ask Claude — intensity
Protein — the non-negotiable
Target 1.6–2.2g of protein per kg of bodyweight daily. At 80kg that's 128–176g. Spread it across 3–4 meals — muscle protein synthesis is limited per meal, so one large serving does not equal four smaller ones across a day.

Prioritise whole food sources: eggs, meat, fish, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, legumes. Protein powder is a convenient supplement, not a replacement.
Calories for your goal
꧁
Building muscle: slight caloric surplus of 200–300 kcal above maintenance. Larger surpluses accelerate fat gain without speeding muscle gain.
꧁
Losing fat: deficit of 300–500 kcal below maintenance. Preserve protein intake — muscle is expensive to rebuild.
꧁
Recomposition: maintenance calories with high protein. Slower, but possible, especially early in training.
Carbohydrates
Carbohydrates are the primary fuel for resistance training. Restricting them too aggressively degrades session quality — you'll lift less, recover slower, and feel worse. Time your largest carb intake around training: before for energy, after for replenishment.
Meal structure
A useful ordering principle: vegetables first, protein second, carbohydrates third. Eating in this sequence blunts blood sugar spikes and supports satiety. It doesn't need to be rigid — the total intake across the day matters more than any single meal's composition.
Hydration
Target ~35ml per kg of bodyweight daily, more on training days. Even mild dehydration (1–2% bodyweight) measurably impairs strength output and cognitive function. Drink water consistently through the day rather than large volumes in one sitting.
Anti-inflammatory basics
Reduce processed foods, refined sugars, and seed oils during training blocks. Ginger and turmeric have modest evidence for reducing exercise-induced inflammation — useful additions to diet, not a substitute for adequate protein and sleep.
⊹ Ask Claude — nutrition
Warmup — dynamic first
A warmup has two jobs: raise core temperature and prime the movement patterns you're about to use. 5–10 minutes of dynamic movement (leg swings, arm circles, world's greatest stretch, band pull-aparts) is enough. Static stretching before lifting reduces force output — save it for after.
Warmup sequence
꧁
Cat-cow — thoracic spine mobilisation
꧁
Scapula raise & press — shoulder blade control
꧁
Cobra & thoracic rotation
꧁
World's Greatest Stretch — hips, thoracic, hamstrings
꧁
Hip 90/90 or Asian squat hold
꧁
Band pull-aparts (before upper body)
Cooldown — static stretching
After training, when the muscles are warm and pliable, is the most effective time to improve flexibility. Hold each stretch 45–60 seconds without bouncing. Target muscles trained that day. Don't push to pain — you should feel tension and mild discomfort, not sharp sensation.
Areas most people neglect
꧁
Hip flexors — tight from sitting, limit squat depth and pelvic position
꧁
Thoracic spine — limited rotation causes shoulder and neck compensation
꧁
Rear delts & external rotators — chronically weak in pressing-heavy programs
꧁
Calves & ankles — restrict squat mechanics more than most realise
Recovery between sessions
Do not train the same muscle group on consecutive days. Muscle protein synthesis remains elevated for 48–72 hours post-training — you need that window for repair. Active recovery (walking, swimming, light mobility) on rest days supports blood flow without adding stress. Sleep 7–9 hours — the majority of growth hormone release occurs during deep sleep stages.
